# Lesson 6: IELTS Speaking Mock — Success, Ambition & Role Models

## Level: B2 → 7.0+
## Duration: 60 minutes
## Format: Full IELTS Speaking Test Simulation

---

## New Format Rationale

From L6 onward, Margo's lessons are **full IELTS Speaking mock tests**. Each lesson follows the exact structure and timing of the real IELTS Speaking test:

- **Part 1** (4-5 min): Introduction + 3 topic sets, scored per-topic timing
- **Part 2** (3-4 min): Random cue card draw, 1-min prep, 2-min speak, follow-up questions
- **Part 3** (4-5 min): Random question set, sequential reveal with increasing abstraction

After the mock: Language Harvest, Band Score Analysis, Correction Board, and homework aligned to the weakest criterion.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 0. Setup (2 min)
- Open `index.html` in browser
- Ensure microphone permissions allowed (if using Web Speech for Part 2 follow-up)
- Slide 1 visible — Margo reads "Before the Test: Band Descriptors" silently

### 1. Part 1 — Introduction & Interview (12 min)

**Introduction Round (2 min):** Exaggerated ID check to settle into English mode.
- "What's your full name?" → "Where are you from?" → "Can I see your ID?"

**Topic Set 1: Work / Study (3 min)**
- 3 questions about Margo's studies. She gives 2-3 sentence answers.
- Timer: 1:30 per topic. Teacher moves on when timer ends or answers become minimal.

**Topic Set 2: Ambition & Goals (3 min)**
- What are your ambitions? Do you set goals? Who influenced you?

**Topic Set 3: Defining Success (3 min)**
- How do you define success? Do successful people share qualities? Success vs happiness?

**Teacher notes:**
- Press "REC" at start of Part 1. Recording indicator stays on.
- Let Margo finish each answer naturally — don't interrupt.
- Only move to next topic after the 1:30 timer or if answers dry up.

### 2. Part 2 — Cue Card (10 min)

**Deal step:** Click "Deal Cards" → 4 face-down cards appear with shuffle animation.
- Margo clicks one card → it flips to reveal the cue card.
- Or click "Re-deal" for a different set.

**Cue card topics (random draw from 4):**
1. Describe a successful person you admire
2. Describe a goal you worked hard to achieve
3. Describe a role model who influenced your choices
4. Describe a time when you helped someone achieve something

**Timing:**
1. Click "Start Prep" → 1:00 preparation timer (Margo makes notes)
2. Auto-transitions to 2:00 speaking timer
3. When speaking ends → follow-up questions appear automatically

**Teacher role:**
- During prep: Don't speak. Let her plan.
- During speaking: Don't interrupt. Nod encouragingly.
- After speaking: Ask the 2 follow-up questions displayed on screen.

### 3. Part 3 — Discussion (12 min)

**Set selection:** Margo (or teacher) clicks one of 3 themed sets:
- 🏆 Nature of Success
- 🏔 Ambition & Motivation
- 🧭 Role Models & Influence

**Sequential reveal:** Questions appear one at a time. Click "Next Question" to advance.

**5 questions per set** with increasing abstraction:
- Q1-2: Personal / general opinion
- Q3-4: Societal / comparative analysis
- Q5: Abstract / speculative

**Discussion frameworks (show on screen for reference):**
- Position → Evidence → Counter → Conclusion
- Past vs Present comparison
- Hypothesis → Condition → Speculation

**Teacher prompts if Margo struggles:**
- "What do you mean by that exactly?"
- "Can you give an example?"
- "Why do you think that is?"

### 4. Language Harvest (8 min)

**Phase 1:** Review the collocation cards on screen. Say each example aloud together.
**Phase 2:** Fill in the "Today's Crop" table with upgrades from Margo's actual responses.
**Phase 3:** Use the Upgrade Builder — type a phrase Margo used, show a Band 7+ alternative.

**Key collocations to target:**
- aspire to, driven by, set one's sights on (ambition)
- milestone, breakthrough, pinnacle (success)
- look up to, emulate, trailblazer (role models)
- accomplish, fulfil, come to fruition (achievement)

### 5. Band Score Analysis (7 min)

**Teacher assesses each criterion (1-9 scale):**
- Fluency & Coherence
- Lexical Resource
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy
- Pronunciation

Adjust the score inputs on screen. The overall band calculates automatically.
The vertical thermometer fills to show the band visually.

**Weekly Target:** Choose ONE criterion to focus on for L7. Tap the chip to lock it in.

### 6. Correction Board (5 min)

- Add specific errors Margo made during the mock
- Categorise by type (Lexical, Grammar, Coherence, Pronunciation)
- Review error chain from prior lessons (L1-L5) — check if old errors resurface
- Clear recurring errors as new weekly targets

### 7. Homework (2 min)

1. **Self-recorded mock** — Same topic, full 3 parts
2. **Band descriptor self-assessment** — Grade all 4 criteria
3. **Vocabulary log** — 5 sentences with topic collocations

## Teacher Reference — Model Answers & Discussion Texts

### Part 1 Model Answers (Speaking Style)

**Topic 1 — Work / Study:**
> Well, I'm currently in my third year at university — I'm studying International Relations. To be honest, it wasn't what I initially planned to do, but I've really grown to love it. What I enjoy most is probably the way it's opened my eyes to how interconnected the world actually is. You know, we tend to see things from our own perspective, but studying IR forces you to consider multiple viewpoints, which I find fascinating.

**Topic 2 — Ambition & Goals:**
> That's a good question. My main ambition, I'd say, is to build a career in diplomacy or international development. I think setting goals is absolutely crucial — without them, you can kind of drift through life without any real direction. As for who's influenced me most, I'd have to say my parents — they've always encouraged me to aim high, but they've also taught me that success isn't just about career achievements. I mean, it's about being a decent person along the way.

**Topic 3 — Defining Success:**
> You know, I've actually thought about this quite a lot. For me personally, success isn't really about money or status — I mean, those things are nice to have, but they're not what I'd consider true success. I think real success is about personal fulfilment and making some kind of positive impact, however small. And interestingly, I've noticed that people who are genuinely successful — they all seem to share qualities like resilience, humility, and a willingness to keep learning.

### Part 2 Sample Responses (Band 7+, Speaking Style)

**Cue Card 1 — Describe a successful person you admire:**
> Right, well, I'd like to talk about my mother, who I honestly think is one of the most remarkable people I know. She grew up in a fairly modest household — her parents were factory workers — but she was determined to build a better life. She worked incredibly hard, earned a scholarship to medical school, and eventually became a surgeon. But here's the thing — what I admire most isn't her professional success. It's how she balanced a demanding career with being a genuinely present parent. I mean, she'd work twelve-hour shifts and still come home to help me with my homework. Her example has shaped my own aspirations enormously. She taught me that success is about lifting other people up along the way.

**Cue Card 2 — Describe a goal you worked hard to achieve:**
> I'd say the goal I've worked hardest for was getting into my current university. I spent months preparing — I mean, it felt like my entire life revolved around entrance exams. I'd wake up early to study before class, then spend evenings writing and rewriting my personal statement. The hardest part was dealing with the self-doubt — constantly questioning whether I was good enough. But I kept going by breaking it down into smaller milestones. Instead of thinking about the final goal, I'd focus on one exam at a time, one essay at a time. When I finally got the acceptance letter, I felt this overwhelming sense of relief — and pride, I suppose. But looking back, what I value most is what the process taught me about resilience and self-discipline. I think those lessons have been far more valuable than the acceptance letter itself.

**Cue Card 3 — Describe a role model who influenced your choices:**
> I'd like to talk about my high school history teacher, Mr Volkov. Before his classes, I thought history was just about memorising dates — pretty dull, to be honest. But he completely changed my perspective. He'd challenge us to question the official narratives and form our own opinions. I remember one lesson where he asked us to write about a historical event from the perspective of the losing side — and that exercise completely changed how I understood history. He was the first person who made me realise that education isn't about getting right answers — it's about asking the right questions. I ended up choosing International Relations largely because of the curiosity he sparked in me. I honestly doubt I'd be where I am today without his influence.

**Cue Card 4 — Describe a time when you helped someone achieve something:**
> I'd like to tell you about a time I helped my younger sister prepare for her final exams. She was struggling with mathematics and had pretty much given up hope. So I created a study plan — nothing too rigid, just breaking down the topics into manageable chunks. We'd spend about two hours every evening working through problems together. And I made a point of celebrating every small improvement. You know, if she solved a problem she'd been stuck on, we'd take a break and have tea together. Gradually, her confidence grew. When she got her results — she'd passed with a grade she'd thought was impossible — she was absolutely overjoyed. I think the main thing I learned was that sometimes people don't need a genius to solve their problems — they just need someone who believes in them and offers consistent support.

### Part 3 Discussion Springboards

*Read these aloud before starting each question set to frame the discussion:*

**Nature of Success:**
> We're surrounded by images of success — luxury cars, corner offices, social media fame — but research suggests these external markers rarely lead to lasting satisfaction. Psychologists distinguish between extrinsic aspirations — wealth, status, image — and intrinsic ones like personal growth and relationships. People who prioritise the latter consistently report higher wellbeing. So when we discuss success today, let's think beyond the surface-level definitions.

**Ambition & Motivation:**
> The interesting thing about ambition is how culturally dependent it is. In some societies, individual ambition is celebrated as a driver of progress. In others, it's viewed more cautiously. And as societies become wealthier, people's ambitions seem to shift from economic security toward self-actualisation. So the question isn't just whether ambition is good or bad — it's about what kind of ambition we're talking about.

**Role Models & Influence:**
> In the past, role models were typically drawn from our immediate community — family members, teachers, local leaders. But social media has completely transformed that landscape. Today, young people are exposed to a global cast of influencers, many of whom achieve prominence through carefully curated personas. This raises interesting questions about authenticity and whether online role models can provide the same guidance as real-world mentors.

### Part 3 Model Answers (Band 7+, Speaking Style)

*One per question — reveal after Margo has attempted her answer.*

**Nature of Success:**
1. *Is success mainly about money and status?* — I think it's a mix, to be honest. Obviously money and status provide security and comfort. But if you only focus on those things, you might end up feeling empty. I mean, I know people who are financially successful but deeply unhappy, and others who have very little but are genuinely content. So for me, true success is about finding meaning and making a positive contribution.
2. *Does society place too much pressure on young people?* — I think to some extent, yes. Social media plays a big role — young people are constantly comparing themselves to carefully curated highlights of other people's lives. Having said that, I also think there's more awareness now about mental health and burnout, so it's not all negative.
3. *How has the definition of success changed?* — I think quite dramatically, actually. A few decades ago, success was largely defined by traditional markers — a stable job, a house, a family. Today, there's much more emphasis on individual fulfilment and self-expression. On the other hand, that also creates pressure to constantly optimise your life.
4. *Why do some achieve success while others with equal talent don't?* — I think it comes down to mindset, opportunity, and resilience. Some people have a growth mindset — they see failure as feedback. But I've noticed that people who persevere and maintain a positive attitude tend to go further, even when the odds are against them.
5. *Can failure be considered a form of success?* — Absolutely. I mean, very few people achieve anything meaningful without experiencing setbacks. Failure teaches you resilience, humility, and often gives you valuable insights that success never could.

**Ambition & Motivation:**
1. *Why are some people more ambitious than others?* — I think it's a combination of personality and environment. Some people are naturally more driven. But your upbringing and cultural context play a huge role — if you grow up where ambition is encouraged, you're more likely to develop it.
2. *Is ambition born or developed?* — I'd say it's mostly developed, actually. If you achieve something and it feels rewarding, you want more of that feeling. If you fail repeatedly without support, your ambition might diminish. So I think it's more nurture than nature.
3. *Are young people more or less ambitious today?* — I think it varies by society. In some countries, young people seem incredibly driven and competitive. But globally, I think pressure is increasing because of social media and economic uncertainty.
4. *What role does fear of failure play?* — It can work in two ways. For some, the fear of embarrassment is a powerful motivator. But for others, it can be paralysing — they become so afraid of failure that they avoid challenges altogether.
5. *Should parents encourage ambition or focus on happiness?* — Both are important. Encouraging ambition without regard for happiness can lead to burnout. But focusing only on happiness without any ambition can lead to complacency. So the ideal is healthy ambition.

**Role Models & Influence:**
1. *Do young people need role models more today?* — In some ways yes. They have access to more information and potential role models online. But real-life role models are becoming less prominent, and online ones often present an unrealistic version of success.
2. *Celebrity vs everyday role model?* — Someone from everyday life, to be honest. Celebrities might be aspirational, but their lives are so removed from reality. A teacher or family member you can actually observe and learn from — that's far more valuable.
3. *Can a person be a role model in one area but fail in others?* — Absolutely, nobody's perfect. I think the important thing is to admire specific qualities rather than idealising the whole person.
4. *How has social media changed role model selection?* — It's had a profound impact. Algorithms curate influencers based on engagement, not substance. This means young people are exposed to those skilled at self-promotion rather than those with genuine expertise.
5. *Should role models be held to higher moral standards?* — That's a tricky one. If someone positions themselves as a public figure, they have a responsibility. But holding them to unrealistic standards isn't fair either. I think the key is transparency and accountability.
