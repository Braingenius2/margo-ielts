02
Part 1: Questions
Technology & Digital Minimalism — personal experience

IELTS Part 1 Questions

  • 1Do you use technology a lot in your daily life? Which devices or apps do you find most useful?
  • 2Have you ever tried to reduce the amount of time you spend on your phone or social media? Why / Why not?
  • 3Do you think people today are too dependent on technology? In what way?
  • 4What kind of technology did you use when you were younger? How has that changed?
  • 5Do you ever switch off your phone completely? How does it make you feel?

Exam Strategy: Do & Don't

✓ DO
  • Give a direct answer first, then extend with a reason
  • Use linking phrases: "Having said that…"
  • Use real examples from your own life
  • Vary vocabulary — don't repeat the question's exact words
  • Aim for 3–5 sentences per answer
✗ DON'T
  • Start every sentence with "Actually"
  • Use filler phrases: "It's a good question…"
  • Give one-word or one-sentence answers
  • Repeat the question back verbatim
  • Switch tense inconsistently mid-narrative

Part 3: Discussion
Abstract questions — use the spoken frameworks to practise natural delivery

🗣 Speaking Tempo — Part 3 Frameworks

Tap each question to see a spoken framework. Use the ▶ button to hear the British voice deliver it, then practise saying it yourself with natural pausing and shorter clauses.

Q1 Do you think technology companies have a responsibility to help users manage their screen time?
Position
"I think companies absolutely have a responsibility here. I mean, these platforms are designed to keep you scrolling — that's not an accident."
Evidence
"Former engineers from Meta and other big tech companies have actually admitted this. They've said the algorithms are deliberately built to be addictive."
Counter
"That said, you could argue it's ultimately down to the individual. It's your choice how you use it."
Conclusion
"But I think given the scale of the problem, we need both — personal responsibility and some kind of regulation."
Q2 How has the widespread use of smartphones changed the way people communicate?
Position
"Smartphones have completely changed how we talk to each other — and honestly, not always for the better."
Evidence
"I mean, think about it — families sitting at the same dinner table texting each other instead of talking. That's become completely normal."
Counter
"On the other hand, they've made it possible to stay in touch with people on the other side of the world. That's something we couldn't do before."
Conclusion
"So it's a mixed picture, really. We're connected to more people, but maybe those connections are shallower."
Q3 Some people believe that digital detoxing is just a trend for wealthy people. Do you agree?
Position
"There's some truth to that. If you're working multiple jobs or rely on your phone for income, switching off isn't really an option."
Evidence
"A lot of gig economy workers depend on their smartphones to make a living. Telling them to take a digital detox isn't just impractical — it's out of touch."
Counter
"But at the same time, the basic idea of digital minimalism — being more intentional with your screen time — that's free. Anyone can do it."
Conclusion
"So I'd say it's not that detoxing is only for the rich — it's more that access to it is uneven."
Q4 What might be the long-term consequences of children growing up with constant access to digital devices?
Position
"I think we're only just starting to understand the long-term effects — and some of the early signs are worrying."
Evidence
"There's research that links heavy screen time in early childhood to shorter attention spans and problems with emotional regulation."
Counter
"At the same time, kids who grow up with technology tend to be more digitally fluent — which is a skill in itself."
Conclusion
"So I think the key difference is between supervised, purposeful use and just passive consumption. That's what matters."
Q5 Should governments regulate how much data technology companies can collect from users?
Position
"In my opinion, yes — I think some level of regulation is not just desirable but necessary."
Evidence
"The GDPR in Europe has shown that you can regulate data collection without killing innovation. It's a good starting point."
Counter
"Critics say too much regulation could push companies to move to countries with weaker rules. And that's a valid concern."
Conclusion
"But given how much power these companies have compared to individual users, I think some form of government oversight is justified."

🗣 Model Answer — Full Recording

~130 words delivered at natural speaking pace. Listen to the full answer, then practise shadowing it section by section.

00:00 / 00:00
So the tech I decided to step back from — well, it was Instagram. I'd been using it for about three years, and honestly, I didn't even realise how much it was affecting me until one evening I noticed I'd spent nearly three hours just scrolling — and I felt worse than before I'd started. The thing was, everyone's life looked so perfectly curated. I kept comparing — you know, my everyday reality to these idealised pictures. And that was doing a lot of damage, looking back. So I deleted the app. Just as an experiment — for a week. And the effect was pretty immediate. I had so much more time. I started reading again, exercising, having proper conversations. That low-level anxiety I hadn't even fully acknowledged — it just started to fade. Would I recommend it? Honestly, yes — if you feel like technology is taking over, try it. But I'm not saying go completely off-grid. Just be more intentional about how you use it. That's really the key.
Speaking Tempo Techniques Used Here:
  • Sentences broken at breath points — average length halved
  • Self-correction modelled: "well," "I kept comparing — you know"
  • Rhetorical question: "Would I recommend it?" — engages the listener
  • Contractions throughout: "I'd", "didn't", "That's"
  • Discourse markers mid-clause: "looking back" placed at end, not fronted
